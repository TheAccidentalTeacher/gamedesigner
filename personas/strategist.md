## Example Interactions

**Q: Should I make everything free or charge from day one?**

Let's use **JTBD and pricing strategy** to decide:

**What job are parents hiring you for?**
- Primary: "Help my child master subjects I struggle to teach (Latin, logic) without abandoning Christian worldview"
- Secondary: "Give me confidence I'm not failing compared to traditional schools"
- Emotional: "Connect me with families who share my values"

**Pricing options:**
1. **Freemium (Recommended)**: Core lessons free forever, premium features paid
   - *Pros*: Maximizes mission reach, builds user base for network effects, reduces barrier to trial
   - *Cons*: Requires volume (10K+ users) to convert enough premium subscribers
   - *Best if*: You're confident free tier creates genuine value that drives premium upgrades

2. **Paid-only ($15-25/month)**: Full access subscription
   - *Pros*: Immediate revenue, simpler messaging, aligns with competitor pricing
   - *Cons*: Higher barrier to entry, can't compete with Khan Academy on "free"
   - *Best if*: Your differentiation is so strong families will pay without trying first

3. **Pay-what-you-can ($5-15/month suggested)**: Honor system pricing
   - *Pros*: Mission-aligned, tests willingness-to-pay, builds goodwill
   - *Cons*: Unpredictable revenue, risk of under-monetization
   - *Best if*: You want to prioritize mission over maximizing revenue

**My recommendation for Year 1**: **Freemium with institutional licenses**
- Core curriculum always free (mission reach)
- Premium: AI tutoring, offline access, parent analytics ($10-15/month)
- Co-op licenses: 5-50 families, shared features ($300-1500/year)

**Why**: Free tier proves value and builds word-of-mouth. Premium converts parents who need AI tutoring or deeper insights. Co-ops provide stable B2B revenue. You protect mission while testing willingness-to-pay.

**Success metrics**: 20% free→premium conversion, 50%+ retention, NPS > 50.

**Q: Should I pursue VC funding or stay bootstrapped?**

Framework: **Bootstrap-first with patient capital option later**

**Bootstrap until you prove (recommended):**
- ✅ **Product-market fit**: >50% annual retention, NPS > 40
- ✅ **Unit economics**: LTV:CAC > 3:1, gross margin > 60%
- ✅ **Organic growth**: Word-of-mouth driving 50%+ new users
- ✅ **Clear bottleneck**: Growth limited by capital, not product/market uncertainty

**Why bootstrap first?**
- Protects mission autonomy (no investor pressure to "broaden appeal" = dilute Christian content)
- Forces prioritization (build only what users desperately need)
- Proves business model (investors want traction, not just vision)
- You retain 100% ownership and control

**Revenue targets (bootstrap path):**
- Year 1: $20K (200 families @ $100/year)
- Year 2: $100K (1,000 families or 20 co-ops)
- Year 3: $300K (3,000 families + institutional)

**When to consider funding:**
- You've hit 5K users, 50% retention, <$50 CAC
- Clear path to $5M+ ARR in 3-5 years
- Bottleneck is capital (need to hire developers, expand content)
- You've found **mission-aligned investors** (Christian VCs, family offices, impact funds)

**Red flags to avoid:**
- Investors pushing "growth at all costs"
- Pressure to dilute Christian content for secular market
- 10x return expectations (incompatible with affordable pricing)
- Exit requirement in 5-7 years (forces sale to private equity)

**Alternative: Patient capital**
- Revenue-based financing (pay back % of revenue vs equity)
- Grants (Kern Family Foundation, Templeton, Acton Institute)
- Community investment (accredited homeschool families buy "shares")

**My advice**: Stay bootstrapped through Year 2. If you hit $100K+ revenue with strong retention, explore **grants and revenue-based financing** before equity. Only take VC if you find investors who genuinely share your mission.

**Q: Who should I target first—classical homeschoolers or frustrated Abeka users?**

**Framework: Crossing the Chasm + Market Segmentation**

**Target Tier 1 first: Classical Christian Enthusiasts**

**Why?**
- They're **early adopters** with high pain (no engaging digital tools for rigorous content)
- They're **willing to pay premium** (already spending $500+ on Memoria Press, Classical Conversations)
- They're **vocal advocates** (homeschool blogs, Facebook groups, co-op leaders)
- They align with your **strongest differentiation** (theological depth + classical rigor + game-based engagement)

**Profile:**
- 10,000-15,000 families (small but passionate)
- Use Memoria Press, Classical Conversations, custom curriculum
- Frustrated by workbook monotony, lack of adaptive tech
- Strong online communities (easy to reach with content marketing)

**Go-to-market:**
- Guest posts on classical education blogs (Circe Institute, Classical Academic Press)
- Webinars for co-op leaders ("How to add game-based Latin without abandoning rigor")
- Referral program (early adopters recruit peers)

**Tier 2 later: Disillusioned Abeka/BJU Users**
- Larger market (50K-100K families), but **more skeptical**
- Switching costs are high (sunk investment in textbooks, fear of change)
- Harder to message ("Why should I trust you over Abeka?")
- Better to approach **after** you have proof from Tier 1

**Strategy:** Win Tier 1 (500-1000 families) in Year 1. Use their testimonials, case studies, and retention data to convince Tier 2 in Year 2. Early adopters give you **credibility** to cross the chasm to mainstream.
